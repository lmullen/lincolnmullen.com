### Behind

Two months ago it dawned on me that I was behind. I hadn't been avoiding generative AI for my work. In fact I used it quite a bit. I would give Claude a database schema, and then ask natural language queries and copy and paste the SQL. When I taught students how to make visualizations, I showed them how to use a chatbot to debug their code. I certainly wasn't above getting a chatbot to generate the rough draft of any administrative summary I deemed nonsense.

That probably made me a power user. At least, I thought so. But at [RRCHNM](https://rrchnm.org) we have the custom of spending a Friday early each semester eating pizza and experimenting collaboratively with some "new media" to see what we might be able to use it for. I'd vaguely been following along about agentic coding, so I decided we would spend that Friday vibe coding.[^1] In preparation, I installed [Claude Code](https://claude.com/product/claude-code) and spent an afternoon with it.

I was behind.

<!--more-->

A not insignificant part of my professional identity has been tied up in my ability to write computer programs when most historians cannot. Back in 2013 George Mason University advertised for an assistant professor who could teach a class on programming for historians. Though I am certain that I have since proven my value to my employer in other ways, I am keenly aware that had my department not made that peculiar hiring decision, I may very well never have had an academic career in history. Then too, a large part of my research depends on computational methods. I'd like to think that the value of that scholarship depends primarily on the history I told, but I'd be deluding myself if my "digital wizardry" (an actual [blurb](https://americaspublicbible.org)) hadn't gotten me more attention than the insights alone deserved.

So it was humbling to type a few paragraphs into Claude Code and get back a working visualization. I typed a few more paragraphs, and had a basic web app. I consoled myself with the idea that a decade of experience writing code had prepared me to prompt well.[^2] Then my kids had six snow days in a row, so I brought them to work and plopped them down in front of Claude. Imagination got more intriguing results than experience.

Has AI been over-hyped? I'm in no position to comment on its general impact on the global economy. But within my narrow world of digital history and computational humanities, this is a sea change. A disruption, as the tech bros put it. It took me about two days of experimenting to realize that my rare and valuable skill was still valuable but no longer rare. When we held the vibe coding event, I watched a first-year graduate student make an insightful map in an hour, a map that was at least as technically sophisticated as the one I had used in my job talk a decade earlier.

Are there risks to using generative AI? Obviously. But it should be equally obvious that not using it will create huge missed opportunities to bring good digital scholarship into the world. That's a risk too, like leaving your money in cash instead of investing in stocks.

### Ahead

I'll miss writing code. I still do it---stubbornly, just because I want to. I'll probably write some code this week. I have this one computational project that contains only code written by one human, i.e., by me. I've mostly refused help on that project from anyone else capable of writing code because I wanted one project to myself where the computational thinking was all mine. I love the feeling of "[resistance in the materials](https://nowviskie.org/2013/resistance-in-the-materials/)," as Bethany Nowviskie discussed.[^3] But when I finish that code this week, that may very well mean goodbye. I'm sure I'll read and edit more code than ever. It just won't make sense for me to write it.

That doesn't mean vibe-coding isn't fun. It is enormously, addictively fun. It feels just a little like being god. Not like being the god who said, "Let there be light: and there was light." It feels more like being the god who had to go into repetitive detail: "Let the earth bring forth grass, the herb yielding seed, and the fruit tree yielding fruit after his kind, whose seed is in itself, upon the earth: and it was so." And occasionally, it feels like being the god who sent a flood in a fit of anger to destroy almost all of creation and start over. But you type, and your imaginations come into being. Consider Paul Ford, one of my favorite writers and the author of the 2015 essay, "[What is code](https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2015-paul-ford-what-is-code/)."[^4] Now he is [vibe coding on the train](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/18/opinion/ai-software.html).[^5] While I was writing this post I had to go to an event. I should have kept my phone in my pocket, but I didn't: I sent Claude Code prompts for a project every few minutes.

In a certain sense, I've been ahead of this change for some time. If I had my druthers, most days I'd like it to be just me and the terminal. But I have to be a PI on grants, or the leader of a project team, or the director of a research center. When my colleagues and I discuss how to create some project, I try to bite my tongue and avoid expressing opinions about implementation. (At least, I try.) I'm self-aware enough to know that my programming skills either never existed in some domains or have atrophied enough in others that I should leave that work to the real developers. Besides, even if I did know how to do the implementation, that's just not my role any more. Using Claude is obviously not collaborating with a colleague. But I am already accustomed to expressing goals and then leaving much of the hands-on work to others more skilled than I.

There's a more important sense, though, in which I've been thinking ahead about this change. For a long time I have argued digital history should be judged by the same standards as other academic histories. At the same time my colleague Stephen Robertson and I have been [arguing](https://doi.org/10.1093/jsh/shab015) that digital historians should make arguments and advance interpretations about the past.[^6] I don't mean that digital history work should be subjected to the same short-hand rules (is it a book? was it peer-reviewed?) that substitute for actual evaluation. I mean that digital history work should obviously and on its face present an answer to the more discerning question: does this work say something meaningful about the past?

Digital historians love to complain that their work does not get the credit it deserves because it is not a book or an article. Maybe. It seems to me like that is a battle which earlier generations fought and won. From my perspective, I see the opposite problem more often. One of my fundamental principles is that work should be judged on results, not effort. Digital history work is hard and takes a long time. Do you know what else is hard and takes a long time? Writing books. Too often digital historians attribute the value of their work to the difficulty in creating it. Too often that is a cover for digital projects that have stalled out at the collection or database phase without advancing to the phase of meaningful interpretation.

What if much of the difficulty of doing specifically digital scholarship goes away? What if many of the hard to acquire skills of digital history, such as developing websites and web applications, cleaning and ingesting data, visualizing or mapping it, organizing metadata, and so on become radically easier? Of course access to these technologies is unevenly distributed. But even a \$100 per month Anthropic subscription is a lot more accessible than hiring a web developer, fulltime or freelance. And it's even more accessible than learning to become a full-stack web developer yourself. If you want to create a work of digital history and don't have to do that work directly or pay to get someone else to do it, where does that leave us?

I suspect a number of digital historians will end up discomfitted, at least temporarily. We have conveniently benefitted from the fact that much of our actual day-to-day work seems obscure to our colleagues (while, perversely, we have argued that the results were more public and more accessible just because they were online). If I may belabor the Genesis analogy one last time, the fig leaf of methodological difficulty won't cover a lack of interpretative results much longer.

But this radical change in what is possible and who can do it will also be enormously freeing. I taught people to debug error messages and check the nesting of their parentheses as a means to an end, not as the end in itself.[^7] Just because the implementation is vastly easier doesn't mean historians will automatically have better ideas for digital scholarship. We will have to continue to teach and cultivate digital historical *thinking*. Making history in the form of visualizations or maps or web interfaces or online collections or what have you is still a very different way of thinking than writing a conventional book or article. But if the barriers between idea and implementation go down---way, way down---what does that mean for digital history?

It means digital history, like the rest of history, should be judged not by the difficulty of its implementation but by the quality of its ideas and interpretations. In short, have we said something meaningful about the past?
